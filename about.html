About Earth's Atmosphere

The Earth's atmosphere is divided into five main layers based on temperature changes. Each layer has unique characteristics and plays an important role in protecting and supporting life on Earth.The Earth’s atmosphere is a thin layer of gases that surrounds our planet and makes life possible. It provides the air we breathe, protects us from harmful radiation from the Sun, and helps regulate the Earth’s temperature. Scientists divide the atmosphere into several layers based on changes in temperature and altitude. Each layer has unique characteristics and plays an important role in protecting and supporting life on Earth.

1. Troposphere

Height: Surface to about 8–15 km

The troposphere is the lowest layer and contains about 75% of the atmosphere’s mass and almost all water vapor. This is where humans live and where all weather occurs. It begins at the Earth’s surface and extends up to about 8 to 15 kilometers, depending on location. This layer contains most of the atmosphere’s air and nearly all of its water vapor, which makes it the place where weather occurs. Clouds, rain, wind, and storms all form in the troposphere, making it essential for supporting life on Earth.

  • Clouds, rain, snow, and storms form here.
  • Temperature decreases as altitude increases.
  • Commercial airplanes usually fly in the upper troposphere.

2. Stratosphere

Height: 15 km to 50 km

The stratosphere is above the troposphere and contains the ozone layer, which absorbs harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the Sun.The stratosphere is the second layer of the Earth’s atmosphere, located above the troposphere. It extends from about 15 kilometers to around 50 kilometers above the Earth’s surface. This layer is important because it contains the ozone layer, which absorbs most of the Sun’s harmful ultraviolet radiation. Unlike the troposphere, temperatures in the stratosphere increase with altitude, making it more stable and less turbulent.

  • Temperature increases with altitude due to ozone absorbing UV rays.
  • Weather balloons and some aircraft fly in this layer.
  • It is very dry with little water vapor.

3. Mesosphere

Height: 50 km to 85 km

The mesosphere is the coldest layer of the atmosphere. Most meteors burn up in this layer before reaching Earth's surface.The stratosphere is the second layer of the Earth’s atmosphere, located above the troposphere. It extends from about 15 kilometers to around 50 kilometers above the Earth’s surface. This layer is important because it contains the ozone layer, which absorbs most of the Sun’s harmful ultraviolet radiation. Unlike the troposphere, temperatures in the stratosphere increase with altitude, making it more stable and less turbulent.

  • Temperatures can drop to -90°C.
  • Meteors create bright streaks of light here.
  • Very thin air makes it difficult to study directly.

4. Thermosphere

Height: 85 km to 600 km

The thermosphere is a very hot layer due to absorption of high-energy solar radiation. Despite high temperatures, it would not feel hot because the air is extremely thin. The thermosphere is the fourth layer of the Earth’s atmosphere, lying above the mesosphere and extending from about 85 kilometers to 600 kilometers above the Earth’s surface. In this layer, temperatures rise significantly due to the absorption of high-energy solar radiation. The thermosphere is also where phenomena like the auroras occur, and it is the region where satellites and the International Space Station orbit the Earth.

  • The International Space Station orbits here.
  • Auroras (Northern and Southern Lights) occur in this layer.
  • Temperature increases dramatically with altitude.

5. Exosphere

Height: 600 km to 10,000 km

The exosphere is the outermost layer and gradually fades into outer space. Particles of gas can escape into space from this region.It contains very few particles, mostly hydrogen and helium, which are so spread out that they can travel hundreds of kilometers without colliding. The exosphere acts as the transition between Earth’s atmosphere and space, gradually fading into the vacuum of space.

  • Mainly composed of hydrogen and helium.
  • Satellites orbit within this layer.
  • It has extremely low air density.
