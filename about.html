About Earth's Atmosphere

The Earth's atmosphere is divided into five main layers based on temperature changes. Each layer has unique characteristics and plays an important role in protecting and supporting life on Earth.

1. Troposphere

Height: Surface to about 8–15 km

The troposphere is the lowest layer and contains about 75% of the atmosphere’s mass and almost all water vapor. This is where humans live and where all weather occurs.

  • Clouds, rain, snow, and storms form here.
  • Temperature decreases as altitude increases.
  • Commercial airplanes usually fly in the upper troposphere.

2. Stratosphere

Height: 15 km to 50 km

The stratosphere is above the troposphere and contains the ozone layer, which absorbs harmful ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the Sun.

  • Temperature increases with altitude due to ozone absorbing UV rays.
  • Weather balloons and some aircraft fly in this layer.
  • It is very dry with little water vapor.

3. Mesosphere

Height: 50 km to 85 km

The mesosphere is the coldest layer of the atmosphere. Most meteors burn up in this layer before reaching Earth's surface.

  • Temperatures can drop to -90°C.
  • Meteors create bright streaks of light here.
  • Very thin air makes it difficult to study directly.

4. Thermosphere

Height: 85 km to 600 km

The thermosphere is a very hot layer due to absorption of high-energy solar radiation. Despite high temperatures, it would not feel hot because the air is extremely thin.

  • The International Space Station orbits here.
  • Auroras (Northern and Southern Lights) occur in this layer.
  • Temperature increases dramatically with altitude.

5. Exosphere

Height: 600 km to 10,000 km

The exosphere is the outermost layer and gradually fades into outer space. Particles of gas can escape into space from this region.

  • Mainly composed of hydrogen and helium.
  • Satellites orbit within this layer.
  • It has extremely low air density.
